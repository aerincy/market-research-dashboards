01 · MARKET OVERVIEW

Global Space Insurance Market — Size & Growth

The space insurance market has grown from a niche specialty product into a multi-billion dollar global industry, driven by the commercialization of space, rising satellite values, and the expansion of launch activity. The market is projected to grow from $4.43B in 2026 to $6.23B by 2030 at a 9.1% CAGR.

03 · COVERAGE TYPE ANALYSIS

Coverage Segments — Where the Risk Lives

Space insurance is structured around three primary coverage phases: pre-launch (ground risk), launch (the highest-risk phase), and in-orbit (operational risk). Each phase has distinct risk profiles, claim frequencies, and relevance to survivable data recording technology.

Coverage Type Distribution (2026)

% of total $4.43B market by coverage phase

Coverage Type — Value & Claim Frequency

Market value vs. estimated annual claims frequency by coverage type

Pre-Launch Coverage

Ground risk during manufacturing, testing & transport
$796M market (18%). Covers physical damage to spacecraft and launch vehicle during ground operations. Claims are relatively infrequent but can be large. Our technology relevance: moderate — data logging during ground testing provides baseline for anomaly detection.

Launch Coverage

Lift-off through initial orbit insertion (typically 1 year)
$1.82B market (41%). The highest-risk phase — launch vehicle failures, separation anomalies, and early orbit failures. Our technology is directly applicable here: the device survives the explosion and transmits data, enabling root cause analysis and supporting insurance claims.

In-Orbit Coverage

Operational satellite insurance throughout mission life
$1.77B market (40%). Covers anomalies and total losses during operational life. Market is shifting as large GEO satellites decline and LEO constellations grow. Our technology relevance: high — continuous data logging throughout mission life provides the forensic record insurers need for complex in-orbit anomaly claims.

07 · REGULATORY ENVIRONMENT

Regulatory Landscape — The Mandate Opportunity

Aviation's universal adoption of black boxes was driven by regulatory mandate, not voluntary adoption. The same dynamic is emerging in space insurance. Germany's proposed Weltraumgesetz, Singapore's new space legislation, and evolving US FAA requirements are creating the conditions for mandatory survivable data recording requirements.

🇺🇸 United States Most Developed
◆FAA Part 450: Mandates third-party liability insurance for all commercial launches. No mandatory in-orbit coverage.
◆NASA Requirements: Mission assurance standards for government payloads include data recording requirements, but no survivable standard exists.
◆Space Force: Commercial-first policy and new Space Systems Command standards are creating de facto data recording requirements for commercial suppliers.
◆Liability Convention: US is a signatory; government indemnifies commercial operators up to $500M for third-party claims.
Mandate Pathway: A single high-profile US failure with unrecoverable data — especially involving loss of life in commercial human spaceflight — could trigger rapid regulatory action requiring survivable data recorders on all licensed vehicles.
🇩🇪 Germany Developing
◆Current Status: No legal requirement for space insurance or government indemnification. Operators rely on voluntary coverage.
◆Proposed Weltraumgesetz: 2024 proposal would mandate liability insurance or financial guarantee. Operator liability capped at 10% of annual turnover, max €50M.
◆ESA Standards: ECSS (European Cooperation for Space Standardization) standards apply to all ESA-funded missions, including data recording requirements.
◆Military Space: €35B military space investment (2026–2030) will likely include data recording requirements for classified satellite programs.
Mandate Pathway: The Weltraumgesetz, once enacted, will create mandatory insurance requirements for all German space operators — and Munich Re / Allianz will likely require survivable data recording as a condition of coverage for high-value missions.
🇸🇬 Singapore Emerging
◆NSAS Launch (April 2026): New National Space Agency of Singapore will establish national space policy, including insurance and liability frameworks.
◆New Space Legislation: Singapore is developing a comprehensive space law expected to include mandatory insurance requirements aligned with international conventions.
◆Lloyd's Asia Hub: Singapore's position as Lloyd's primary APAC hub means insurance standards set here effectively apply across the region.
◆STDP Program: S$60M Space Technology Development Programme actively funds technologies that improve space safety and mission assurance.
Mandate Pathway: Singapore's new space legislation is being written now — there is a window to engage NSAS and advocate for survivable data recording as a standard requirement, establishing a regulatory mandate before competitors are aware of the opportunity.

The Aviation Analogy — A Regulatory Mandate Is Inevitable

Aviation black boxes were not adopted voluntarily. They were mandated by the FAA following the 1958 Aviation Safety Act, after a series of crashes where the cause could not be determined. The space industry is at an identical inflection point. The ViaSat-3 and Inmarsat I6 F2 failures of 2023 — combined with the emerging commercial human spaceflight sector — are creating the political and regulatory conditions for a space black box mandate.
