The Problem & Market Opportunity

Thank you for your time today. I want to start with a simple question: when a $500 million satellite fails in orbit, or a commercial launch vehicle explodes on the pad, what data survives to tell us why?

Today, the answer is almost nothing. The space industry is experiencing a historic boom — 317 orbital launches in 2025 alone[1] — yet it still has no equivalent of the aviation black box. Every catastrophic failure is, in data terms, a total loss. That means the same failures repeat, insurance premiums stay high, and the industry cannot learn at the speed it needs to.

We have built the solution. Our technology is a distributed system of hardened "data neurons" — placed inline throughout a vehicle's systems — that survive blast, fire, and atmospheric reentry, then transmit their data via GPS after an incident. We have already proven this in a real-world launch explosion. The black box survived. The data was recovered.

The market opportunity this creates spans five verticals and a combined TAM of $2.18 billion[2], with an immediate, serviceable market of $852 million across the United States, Germany, and Singapore.

Total Addressable Market (TAM) — Value Theory Approach

We size our TAM using the Value Theory approach — not a top-down market report, but a bottom-up calculation of the economic value our technology creates for each customer. This is the methodology that sophisticated deep-tech investors expect, because it forces us to justify every number.

The core logic is this: with 317 orbital launches in 2025[1] and a historical failure rate of approximately 5%[3], the industry faces roughly 15 catastrophic failures per year. At an average mission value of $200 million, and assuming our technology can prevent or mitigate 25% of those losses, the annual value to space operators alone is $750 million.

On top of that, the global space insurance market — valued at $4.43 billion in 2026[4] — benefits directly from our technology. Better post-failure data means faster claims, lower litigation costs, and more accurate actuarial models. We conservatively value that systemic benefit at 7.5% of the insurance market, or $332 million.

Add in the near-term terrestrial markets — autonomous shipping and unmanned submersibles — and the long-term Vision TAM in asteroid mining and the lunar economy, and you arrive at a combined TAM of $2.182 billion.

Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM) — By Country & Segment

The SAM narrows our focus to what we can realistically serve with our current technology and resources. We have chosen three markets strategically: the United States as our validation market, Germany as our European anchor, and Singapore as our Asia-Pacific gateway.

A critical insight that distinguishes our market model: we have identified two distinct buyer categories in each geography. The first is space operators — launch providers, satellite manufacturers, government agencies — who buy our hardware to protect their vehicles. The second is space insurers — Munich Re, AXA XL, Lloyd's Asia — who have a direct financial incentive to mandate, subsidize, or co-fund our installation in exchange for better claims data and lower premiums.

This dual-buyer dynamic is a powerful commercial accelerant. Insurers can drive adoption faster than any direct sales effort by making our device a condition of coverage — exactly the mechanism that made aviation black boxes universal. Our combined SAM across these three geographies is $852 million.

Space Insurance Market — Deep Dive

The space insurance market deserves special attention because it is both a customer segment and a market accelerant. The global market is valued at $4.43 billion in 2026 and is growing at 9.1% CAGR, reaching $6.23 billion by 2030.[4] North America holds 41% of that market, Europe 26%, and Asia-Pacific 24%.

The key insight is that space insurers are not passive participants in this market. They are active stakeholders with a direct financial incentive to improve post-failure data quality. Every catastrophic failure that goes unexplained costs them in litigation, extended claims processing, and inaccurate risk modeling. Our technology solves all three problems simultaneously.

The aviation parallel is instructive and compelling: the International Civil Aviation Organization mandated flight data recorders after a series of unexplained crashes in the 1950s and 60s. As the space industry matures and regulatory bodies like the FAA, ESA, and the new Singapore NSAS develop safety frameworks, a similar mandate for survivable data recording is not merely possible — it is structurally inevitable.

Vision TAM — Asteroid Mining & Lunar Economy (2030–2040)

I want to close the market sizing discussion with what I call the Vision TAM — not because it is speculative, but because it illustrates the structural inevitability of our technology's role in the future of the off-world economy.

The asteroid mining market is projected to grow from $2.05 billion today to $5.42 billion by 2030 at a 21.4% CAGR.[5] The commercial lunar economy is forecast to surpass $170 billion by 2040.[6] These are environments where a catastrophic failure of a mining vehicle or a lunar habitat is not just a financial loss — it is a mission-ending, potentially life-threatening event where physical investigation is impossible.

In these environments, our technology is not a component. It is foundational infrastructure. The same regulatory dynamic that made black boxes mandatory in aviation will play out in the lunar economy — and we intend to be the established standard before that mandate arrives. Our Vision TAM from these two markets alone is $665 million.[5,6]
